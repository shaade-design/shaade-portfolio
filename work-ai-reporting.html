AI Reporting Suite — designing AI that PMs will trust

UserVoice had more feedback data than any PM could process. The design challenge wasn't building smarter AI — it was building AI that a PM would stake their roadmap on.

My Role

Product design, systems design, user research, marketing

Scope

Theme Explorer · Impact Reports · Component library · Launch page

Outcome

~99% faster discovery · Anchor for AI subscription tier

Type

0 → 1 product design

The Problem

Data wealth. Insight poverty.

Product managers were sitting on thousands of pieces of user feedback — and defaulting to gut feelings anyway. The barrier to deep analysis was so high that teams either ignored the data or spent half a day manually building a spreadsheet to present a single insight to leadership. Neither path led to confident decisions.

The input

Thousands of unstructured ideas, votes, and comments — more feedback than any PM could read in a week, let alone synthesize into a roadmap argument.

The workaround

Power users were exporting rows to spreadsheets, manually tagging themes, and building pivot tables. A process only accessible to the most data-fluent admins.

The goal

Move UserVoice from a feedback storage tool to a strategic partner. Reduce time-to-insight from hours to seconds. Shift the conversation from "what was said" to "what should we do."

What Research Taught Us

Our first hypothesis was half right.

We built a sophisticated graph-forward Theme Explorer based on a reasonable assumption: PMs are analytical, they want to see the data. Testing with customers at Procore, Zendesk, Greenhouse, and others returned a consistent verdict — impressive, but not actionable. Three findings reshaped everything.

The Complexity Ceiling

The Theme Explorer felt like a pivot table. Powerful for data scientists, too dense for the average PM. 4 out of 4 prototype users couldn't explain what "Activity" meant without prompting.

"The accuracy doesn't seem quite right... erroneous ideas inflate the value on the graphics."

The Desire for Control

Users didn't want a black box. They explicitly asked for human-in-the-loop controls — seed themes, merge categories, remove irrelevant ideas. AI that works silently isn't trusted.

This became a design mandate across the entire suite: every AI output needed a human override.

The "So What?" Factor

Users loved seeing revenue and urgency trends. They couldn't translate those graphs into a roadmap narrative. A PM at Zendesk was already exporting to PowerPoint weekly — the workaround was the product gap.

She needed shareable synthesis, not a better exploration interface.

Design Evolution

From spaghetti chart to structured narrative.

The visual shift between Theme Explorer and Impact Reports isn't just aesthetic — it's a design argument. Every element that changed reflects something research taught us about how PMs actually make decisions.

Key Design Decisions

Four decisions that defined the product's character.

Every design decision in Impact Reports was filtered through one question: will a PM feel safe putting this in front of their VP? Here are the four that mattered most.

Icon-anchored metrics — front-loading signal before the narrative

Key metrics block Ideas, voters, account coverage, importance, urgency — each with a custom icon

Metric block at the top of every report — the PM knows urgency before reading a word

In the Theme Explorer, urgency was a column in a table. In Impact Reports, I moved urgency, revenue, vote count, and account coverage into a fixed metric block at the very top, each anchored with an icon developed for that specific metric type.

The icon does cognitive pre-work. A PM can register a high urgency score before they've engaged with any text. Icons earn their place when they replace a step.

Design principle

Icons earn their place when they replace a step

If an icon only labels what the text already says, remove it. It earns its place when it lets the user skip a cognitive lookup entirely.

Color-coded sections — visual index for non-linear reading

Full Impact Report — all 5 sections Pain Points (orange), Value to Users (green), Challenge the Hypothesis (blue), Research Questions (purple), Feature Announcement (amber)

Five sections, each a distinct color — the report can be navigated by sight, not just by scrolling

A PM presenting in a meeting shouldn't have to scroll and read to jump to the right section. Color makes the report visually indexed — each section is instantly recognizable at a glance.

Constraint held throughout: color reinforces, never carries. Every section has a text label and an icon too, so the design works for color-blind readers and in greyscale print.

Editability as a trust mechanism — not a safety valve

Full Impact Report — 5 editable sections Each section editable after generation — PM applies judgment on top of AI synthesis

Every section editable post-generation

AI-generated content has a trust problem: if a PM can't put their fingerprints on the output, they won't stand behind it. Every section of an Impact Report is editable after it's generated — not as a workaround, but as the core trust mechanism.

I also addressed the "black box" gap by anchoring every AI narrative to the raw data beneath it — idea count, vote count, account coverage. The text is interpretive. The numbers are not. The AI does the synthesis. The numbers do the verifying. The PM applies the judgment.

The tradeoff

Editability vs. canonical output

Two reports on the same data can look completely different. We chose editability over consistency — because a report a PM can't own is a report they won't use.

Championing "Challenge the Hypothesis" — a section I had to argue for

One of the five AI-generated sections in every Impact Report was my pitch. I advocated for a section that generates research prompts specifically designed to poke holes in the PM's own hypothesis.

The problem: confirmation bias. A feedback tool that only surfaces evidence for your existing point of view isn't a decision tool — it's a mirror. The tool had to be honest enough to surface the counterargument.

This required internal advocacy. It felt counterintuitive to add friction to a product designed to reduce analysis paralysis. The argument I made: strategic friction is different from cognitive overload.

Challenge the Hypothesis section AI-generated research prompts for testing the user's assumptions

"Challenge the Hypothesis" — the section PMs cite most in testimonials

Why it works

Trust through honesty

A tool that only confirms what you already believe loses credibility fast. This section is what makes the AI feel like a smart colleague, not a yes-machine.

Systems Design

Building the component library that made it all consistent.

Impact Reports shipped with a full component library built from scratch — not inherited from the Theme Explorer. Every element had to work across the in-app report, a PDF export, a print state, and the share modal. That constraint forced decisions that made the whole system more coherent.

Color System

Consistent identity across every surface

Each section's color had to read correctly in-app, in PDF, and in print (greyscale). The palette passes contrast checks in all three contexts — the color system is doing accessibility work, not just brand work.

Iconography

Custom icons for five metric types

Revenue, votes, account coverage, importance, urgency each got a dedicated icon — distinctive enough to recognize at small sizes, consistent enough to feel like a system.

Component States

Six states per section component

Default, loading, collapsed, expanded, editing, and shared-view states for every report section. The shared-view state required a separate read-only design that preserved the format without exposing edit controls.

The component library wasn't documentation — it was the mechanism that made the PDF, the print state, and the share modal feel like the same product.

Outcome

From gut feelings to data-backed decisions.

The efficiency gain was significant, but the more meaningful shift was qualitative — PMs moved from "feature debates" to "problem-solving discussions," using the suite to upend roadmap assumptions when the data didn't support them. Impact Reports became the anchor for UserVoice's AI subscription tier.

Audits that took a half-day to assemble now refine in about 60 seconds — ~99% faster
1 in 4 reports was shared with colleagues — a personal tool became a team artifact
Teams ran 20–30 reports a week at steady state — habitual use, not just exploration

The sharing number tells the trust story more clearly than any other metric. A PM who shares a report is a PM willing to put their name next to the AI's output. That's not just adoption — that's the trust problem solved.

Reflection

What I'd carry forward.

Build for the moment of sharing, not just the moment of use. The 25% sharing rate was the signal we hadn't designed for explicitly — and it turned out to be the most meaningful outcome. A report that travels beyond the person who ran it validates the format, the trust model, and the AI's output all at once. I now think about "who else will see this?" as a primary design constraint, not an afterthought.

Feedback is a river, not a lake. Impact Reports is a snapshot — you run it, present it, it ages. The next version needs to surface the delta automatically: not just "here's your report," but "here's what shifted since the last one." The insight doesn't freeze when the report is generated. The tool shouldn't either.
